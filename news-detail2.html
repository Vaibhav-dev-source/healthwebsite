Introducing a new healing process

We asked readers to choose up to three topics from 25 covered in the Best Practices feature. The top 10 most-selected topics include using scribes to assist physicians, payers publishing service costs, and strategies for streamlining the discharge process.
Top 10 Best Practices chosen by Modern Healthcare readers

  • Collaboration to Reduce Readmissions: Hospitals using provider and community collaborations to encourage self-management are significantly lowering readmission rates.
  • Telehealth to Enhance Patient Outcomes: Telehealth platforms that engage patients pre- and post-surgery are yielding better results than traditional methods.
  • Stopping Hard-to-Treat Infections: Hospitals are using weekly emails with case updates and action plans to control the spread of infections.
  • Medical Scribes to Improve Care: Medical scribes help reduce patient wait times and foster more patient-centered care.
  • Software to Prevent Misdiagnoses: Computer-assisted diagnosis technology boosts diagnostic accuracy, lowers costs, and improves patient outcomes.
  • Transparent Service Costs: Insurers are creating searchable databases to show service costs to the public.
  • Streamlining Discharge Processes: Hospitals are using discharge planning and checklists to ease patient flow and reduce bottlenecks.
  • Targeted Support for Patients: The Patient Activation Measure helps health systems identify patients ready to engage in care and those needing additional support.
  • Leadership Development: Hospitals are establishing formal programs to develop and retain future leaders.
  • Nursing Residency Programs: These programs help meet the demand for trained nurses while cutting hospital costs.

    • Modern healthcare today

    Modern healthcare has undergone significant transformation due to advancements in technology. The growing consumerization culture, where patients can easily shop around for services, is reshaping the landscape. Medical practice managers must adapt by developing strategies that ensure high-quality patient care while remaining financially viable. For decades, technology has been integral to medicine, powering specialist equipment like MRI scanners and X-ray machines. Below are key features of modern healthcare and strategies your practice can adopt to navigate challenges and seize new opportunities.

    • Improved doctor-patient communication
    • Efficient recording of information in real-time
    • More collaboration
    • Better connectivity with Internet of Things (IoT)
    • The rise of wearable tracking devices
    Improved doctor-patient communication

    Improving patient satisfaction scores should be a top focus for medical practices. The shift towards patient-centered care highlights the need for doctors to find new, effective ways to communicate with their patients. Patient engagement should go beyond traditional office visits. Healthcare practitioners can look to other industries, like retail, for inspiration and explore using tools like chatbots to address common patient inquiries. These chatbots can be developed and managed by healthcare professionals. Facilitating such communication can include online triage systems to guide patients to the appropriate care.

    Efficient real-time information recording is crucial for modern healthcare.
    Modern healthcare relies heavily on data, but it’s only valuable if it’s accurate and delivered in real-time. Recording errors can lead to misdiagnoses and incorrect treatments. In the past, doctors manually recorded information on paper charts, which had to be circulated among different healthcare providers. This increased the risk of misplaced charts, especially when patients saw multiple doctors, such as locums. Paper-based records often relied on patients’ memories to keep their information up-to-date.
    Better connectivity with Internet of Things (IoT)
    The IoT refers to physical devices, known as connected or 'smart' devices, that are embedded with software, sensors, and electronics to collect and exchange data. With the IoT healthcare market expected to reach $136.8 billion globally by 2021, its adoption is growing rapidly in modern healthcare. The expansion of the IoT is driven by the following factors:
  • An increase in the number of wearable devices.
  • Higher rates of chronic diseases.
  • Better access to high-speed internet.
  • Expectations of cheaper treatment and disease management.
    • The IoT has various applications in modern healthcare, including remote monitoring and medical device integration. By leveraging the IoT, healthcare providers aim to boost patient engagement and enhance the delivery of care. Companies like Stanley Healthcare are utilizing the IoT to support hospitals with real-time location services for tracking staff, patients, and medical devices. Stanley Healthcare’s IoT solutions also enable environmental monitoring, such as checking the temperature of medication storage fridges.

    A key challenge healthcare professionals face with the IoT is managing the vast amount of data collected from devices. Simply gathering data without a clear strategy for utilizing it is a missed opportunity to enhance patient care. To ensure the IoT is effective in modern healthcare, medical professionals must develop a plan to transform data into actionable insights.

    The Rise of Wearable Tracking Devices
    It’s reported that nearly a quarter of Americans own wearable devices, which are commonly used to track sleep, exercise, and heart rates. This aspect of modern healthcare highlights consumers taking a proactive approach by using wearable devices paired with smartphones to collect and store health data. While wearable technology is gaining popularity, some believe adoption rates would increase if doctors could utilize the device data to improve patient outcomes.
    Evolving Healthcare

    Wearable devices have the potential to significantly impact patient health. However, the full effects of wearable technology on modern healthcare are not yet fully understood. Numerous studies are underway to explore how wearables can improve health outcomes. For example, The Orthopaedic Institute at Geisinger Health System will partner with Universal Research Solutions to conduct a study capturing real-time data from patients. One key aim of this study is to determine how wearable data can help healthcare professionals make more informed decisions.
